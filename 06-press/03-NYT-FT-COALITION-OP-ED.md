**Working title:** *Truth needs infrastructure*

---

We write as the founding board of a nonprofit foundation, the LedgerProof Foundation, formed last week to publish, maintain, and operate a single piece of public infrastructure: an open cryptographic standard for proving that a document exists, who produced it, and how it has changed — verifiable by anyone, anywhere, without trusting any single company, government, or authority. We do not write as advocates for one technology. We write as five people whose professional lives — in entertainment, in journalism, in finance, in operations, and in cryptographic systems — have brought us to the same conclusion from very different directions.

The conclusion is this. Across every field we touch, the question *is this document real* has, in the last two years, become the question that determines outcomes. It determines whether a wire transfer is recoverable. It determines whether a piece of evidence is admissible. It determines whether a piece of journalism can be defended in court. It determines whether a regulatory disclosure is timely. It determines whether an artificial-intelligence system's output can be trusted by the human who is about to act on it.

The question is no longer answerable by inspection. A document showing the marks of human authorship is no longer evidence that a human produced it. A document carrying a vendor's authentication metadata is no longer evidence that the vendor produced it; the metadata is stripped on every major distribution channel within seconds. A document timestamped by a database is no longer evidence of the time the document came into being; the timestamp is a row in a database that another row can rewrite.

This is the documentary economy we now operate in. It is the economy that produced, last year, the largest single-firm wire-fraud loss in the history of the American banking system, traceable to a one-character change in a single PDF whose origin no party could independently verify. It is the economy that has, in the past eighteen months, produced multiple criminal convictions overturned on appeal because the evidentiary chain of custody could not be established to the standard a free society's courts properly require. It is the economy in which working journalists, our colleagues at the Columbia Journalism School and elsewhere, now spend an increasing share of their workday determining whether a document handed to them by a source is what the source represents it to be, with tools designed for an era when synthetic content did not exist.

This is not a hypothetical condition. We are not warning of a future risk. We are reporting a present cost. The cost is paid every day by the institutions that depend on the integrity of the record, which is all of them.

The response that the European Union has begun, with Article 50 of the AI Act enforcing in twenty-three days, is one regulatory response. It is the right response. It is also incomplete. The Commission's own draft Code of Practice acknowledges, in writing, that no single marking technique now in production satisfies the regulation. The Article 50 regime is the floor, not the ceiling, of what the documentary economy now requires.

What the documentary economy requires is, in plain English, the same thing that the financial economy received in the nineteenth century from the institutions that built clearing-houses and the conventions of certified bookkeeping; the same thing the electrical economy received in the twentieth century from Underwriters Laboratories and the listing-mark system; the same thing the internet economy received in the 1990s from the SSL padlock and the certificate-authority architecture beneath it. The documentary economy requires *infrastructure*. Public, neutral, open, verifiable, free at the point of verification, and not under the commercial control of any single party who can be coerced, fail, or simply disappear.

This infrastructure does not yet exist at the scale the moment requires. It is being built, in pieces, by the Internet Engineering Task Force, by the Coalition for Content Provenance and Authenticity, by individual standards bodies, by the open-source community, and by a small number of nonprofit operators. The LedgerProof Foundation joins that effort today as one contributor, releasing one open specification (the LedgerProof Receipt, version 1.0) along with the reference implementations and the public verifier that the specification requires to mean anything. The specification is published under Creative Commons. The implementations are MIT-licensed. The verifier will not now or ever charge for verification. The Foundation, as a nonprofit corporation, exists to keep that verifier operating, free, indefinitely.

We are not asking the institutions whose records matter to adopt one specification. We are asking them to recognize that the question — *is this document real* — has become a question with stakes high enough that it can no longer be answered by trusting the holder. The answer requires infrastructure. The infrastructure must be public. The public infrastructure must outlast the institutions that build it. And the moment to build it is now, because the moment we are entering is not one in which the cost of not having it will be paid in some abstract future. It is being paid this quarter, in every business, every court, and every newsroom we know.

We have signed our names to a document we have anchored on Bitcoin called *The Provenance Manifesto*. Other signatures, from inside and outside the boards of the institutions we serve, will follow. We invite them. The work is open. The infrastructure is public. The cost of being wrong about whether a document is real has become higher than the cost of marking it. There is no good reason not to begin.
