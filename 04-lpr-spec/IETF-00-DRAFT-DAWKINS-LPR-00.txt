   A Bitcoin-Anchored SCITT Transparency Profile: The LedgerProof
                       Receipt (LPR), Version 1.0
                   draft-dawkins-scitt-lpr-00


Abstract

   This document specifies the LedgerProof Receipt (LPR), version 1.0,
   as a profile of the IETF Supply Chain Integrity, Transparency, and
   Trust (SCITT) architecture [I-D.ietf-scitt-architecture].  LPR
   defines an open cryptographic format that binds a document or
   AI-generated artifact to its asserted authorship, its precise moment
   of finalization, and its chain of subsequent modification, in a form
   independently verifiable by any party without reliance on a trusted
   custodian.  LPR receipts are anchored, through Merkle-tree
   aggregation following the construction of RFC 6962, on the Bitcoin
   blockchain, which functions as the SCITT Transparency Log substrate.

   The specification is intended for use in document provenance,
   AI-generated-content disclosure under regulatory frameworks such as
   the European Union Artificial Intelligence Act Article 50,
   audit-evidence integrity, and the cryptographic accountability of
   autonomous AI-agent tool calls under the Model Context Protocol.
   The Long-Horizon profile defined in this document provides a
   migration path to post-quantum cryptography using composite
   ML-DSA-65+Ed25519 signatures [I-D.ietf-lamps-pq-composite-sigs]
   for receipts intended to remain verifiable beyond the estimated
   cryptographically-relevant quantum computing (CRQC) threat horizon.

1.  Introduction

   The integrity of the documentary record is no longer self-evident.
   In contemporary practice, a document held by a custodial system can
   be modified after the fact by the custodian, by an insider with
   sufficient privilege, or by an external actor who has obtained that
   privilege, without leaving evidence accessible to outside parties.
   The asserted authorship of a document, including AI-generated
   content, may be presented under a claim the document itself cannot
   independently corroborate.  Timestamp manipulation, vendor-dependent
   verification, and the long-term decay of cryptographic primitives
   compound these threats.

   This document defines the LedgerProof Receipt (LPR), version 1.0,
   as a profile of the IETF SCITT architecture.  LPR receipts function
   as SCITT Statements wrapped in a SCITT-compatible envelope; the
   Foundation-operated calendar server functions as a SCITT
   Transparency Service, with the Bitcoin blockchain as the backing
   append-only log.  This positioning allows LPR to interoperate with
   other SCITT profiles, including [I-D.kamimura-scitt-vcp], while
   providing the unique properties of Bitcoin-anchored verification:
   permissionless, custodian-independent, and provably ordered.

   The specification also defines a Long-Horizon profile that adds
   composite ML-DSA-65+Ed25519 signatures to receipts intended to
   remain verifiable beyond the estimated CRQC threat horizon, and a
   High-Assurance profile for legal-submission and BFSI use cases.

   This document defines the LedgerProof Receipt (LPR), version 1.0:
   an open cryptographic format that binds an artifact's content hash,

   an authorship signature, a precise timestamp, a cryptographic chain
   to predecessor receipts, and a Merkle inclusion proof anchored on
   the Bitcoin blockchain.  Verification proceeds without trust in any
   single custodian, vendor, or authority.

   LPR is intentionally composable with established work, including
   OpenTimestamps [OTS], the Coalition for Content Provenance and
   Authenticity (C2PA), the VeritasChain Verifiable Content Protocol
   [I-D.kamimura-scitt-vcp], and the Microsoft Agent Governance Toolkit
   [MSAGT].  It is intended to be used as a Bitcoin-anchored SCITT
   profile that complements rather than competes with these efforts.

   nonprofit corporation organized under U.S. Internal Revenue Code
   Section 501(c)(3), under the Creative Commons Attribution 4.0
   International license.  Reference implementations in Python and
   TypeScript are published under the MIT license.


2.  Conventions and Definitions

   The key words "MUST", "MUST NOT", "REQUIRED", "SHALL", "SHALL NOT",
   "SHOULD", "SHOULD NOT", "RECOMMENDED", "NOT RECOMMENDED", "MAY", and
   "OPTIONAL" in this document are to be interpreted as described in
   BCP 14 [RFC2119] [RFC8174] when, and only when, they appear in all
   capitals, as shown here.

   For the purposes of this document, the following terms apply:

   Artifact:  Any byte sequence with a stable canonical form that can
       be hashed under SHA-256.  Typical artifacts include documents,
       images, AI model outputs, structured data files, and the
       canonical serializations of JSON-RPC tool-call invocations.

   Receipt:  An LPR data structure as defined in Section 3.

   Authorial Chain:  A sequence of receipts sharing a common trace_id,
       each receipt's chain.prev_receipt_hash field referring to the
       SHA-256 of the canonical encoding of the immediately preceding
       receipt in the sequence.

   Anchor:  The act of including a receipt's Merkle leaf hash in a
       Merkle tree whose root is committed to the Bitcoin blockchain
       via an OP_RETURN transaction output.

   Verifier:  Any party in possession of a receipt, the underlying
       artifact bytes (or a hash equivalent), and connectivity to any
       Bitcoin node sufficient to verify the OP_RETURN containing the
       receipt's anchor.

3.  Receipt Structure

3.1.  Required Fields

   A conformant LPR v1.0 receipt MUST be a canonical CBOR object
   ([RFC8949]) containing all of the following top-level fields:

      lpr_version        unsigned integer; MUST be 1 for v1.0.
      receipt_id         UUID v7 ([RFC9562]).
      trace_id           UUID v7; MAY equal receipt_id for the first
                         receipt in an authorial chain.
      timestamp_ns       64-bit signed integer; nanoseconds since the
                         Unix epoch (1970-01-01T00:00:00Z).
      timestamp_iso      string; RFC 3339 ([RFC3339]) representation
                         in UTC, nanosecond precision.
      artifact           map containing:
         content_hash       32-byte SHA-256 of the canonical artifact
                            bytes.
         hash_algo          string; MUST be "SHA-256" for v1.0.
         content_type       string; MIME type or named profile.
         content_bytes      unsigned integer; length of the artifact.
      authorship         map containing:
         actor_type         string; MUST be one of "HUMAN", "AI_MODEL",
                            "HYBRID", "INSTITUTION".
         actor_id           string; public-key, DID, or named entity.
         actor_assertion    string; the assertion the actor is making.
         tool_chain         array; optional list of tool descriptors.
      chain              map containing:
         prev_receipt_hash  32-byte SHA-256 of the canonical CBOR
                            encoding of the preceding receipt in the
                            authorial chain, or null for the first
                            receipt.
      signature          map containing:
         sig_algo           string; MUST be "Ed25519" for v1.0.
         sig_bytes          64-byte Ed25519 signature ([RFC8032]).
         signer_pubkey      32-byte Ed25519 public key.
      anchor             map containing:
         merkle_leaf_hash   32-byte SHA-256 leaf hash of the receipt
                            in the daily Merkle tree.
         anchor_status      string; "PENDING" or "ANCHORED".
         btc_txid           hex string; present iff ANCHORED.
         btc_block_height   unsigned integer; present iff ANCHORED.
         merkle_path        array of 32-byte hashes; present iff
                            ANCHORED.

3.2.  Signature Scope

   The Ed25519 signature MUST be computed over the canonical CBOR
   encoding of the receipt object with both the signature field and
   the anchor field omitted.  This separates signing (which occurs at
   receipt creation) from anchoring (which occurs in a subsequent
   batch operation and adds the anchor field to a receipt that is
   otherwise complete and signed).


3.3.  Canonical Encoding

   The canonical form of a receipt MUST be the deterministic CBOR
   encoding under [RFC8949] Section 4.2, with definite-length items,
   sorted map keys (length-then-lexicographic), and integers encoded
   in their shortest form.

   Implementations MAY represent receipts in JSON for transport in
   contexts that do not support CBOR.  The SHA-256 hash of a receipt
   for purposes of chain linkage MUST always be computed over the
   canonical CBOR form.  An equivalent JSON serialization MUST use
   the JSON Canonicalization Scheme ([RFC8785]).

   All numeric values in the JSON form MUST be encoded as strings to
   avoid precision loss in implementations that map JSON numbers to
   IEEE 754 binary64.  The CBOR form preserves numeric types
   natively.


4.  Chain Semantics

   Receipts sharing a common trace_id form an authorial chain
   representing a sequence of related authorial events.

   The first receipt in a chain MUST set chain.prev_receipt_hash to
   null.  Every subsequent receipt sharing the same trace_id MUST set
   chain.prev_receipt_hash to the 32-byte SHA-256 of the canonical
   CBOR encoding of the immediately preceding receipt (with that
   receipt's signature and anchor fields included in the encoding).

   This construction provides:

   o  Insertion detection: inserting a receipt into the middle of an
      existing chain breaks the chain because the receipt following
      the insertion has prev_receipt_hash referring to a hash that no
      longer matches the inserted receipt.

   o  Deletion detection: deleting a receipt from the middle of an
      existing chain breaks the chain because the receipt following
      the deletion has prev_receipt_hash referring to a hash for
      which no receipt can be produced.


5.  Anchoring

5.1.  Substrate

   The default and REQUIRED anchoring substrate for LPR v1.0 is the
   Bitcoin main chain.  An LPR v1.0 receipt with anchor_status =
   "ANCHORED" MUST reference a Bitcoin transaction on the main chain.

   Future LPR profiles MAY define additional anchoring substrates
   (e.g., Bitcoin testnet for development; alternative public chains
   for jurisdictional purposes).  Any non-default substrate MUST be
   distinguished by a profile-specific OP_RETURN prefix.


5.2.  Merkle Aggregation

   Receipts produced during a given anchor window MUST be aggregated
   into a Merkle tree following the construction of [RFC6962]
   Section 2.1:

      Leaf(D)     =  SHA-256(0x00 || D)
      Node(L, R)  =  SHA-256(0x01 || L || R)

   The domain-separating prefixes (0x00 and 0x01) are REQUIRED.

   The leaf data D for a given receipt is the canonical CBOR
   encoding of the receipt with the anchor field omitted.  The
   receipt's anchor.merkle_leaf_hash MUST equal Leaf(D) for that D.


5.3.  OP_RETURN Format

   The anchor transaction's OP_RETURN output MUST contain exactly
   36 bytes, in the following form:

      0..3:   ASCII "LPR1" (0x4c 0x50 0x52 0x31)
      4..35:  32-byte Merkle root

   The 4-byte prefix distinguishes LPR anchors from other OP_RETURN
   data and from OpenTimestamps [OTS] anchors (which use a distinct
   prefix).

6.  Profiles

6.1.  Core Profile

   The LPR v1.0 Core profile requires SHA-256, Ed25519, CBOR
   canonical encoding [RFC8949], RFC 6962 Merkle aggregation, and
   Bitcoin mainnet OP_RETURN anchoring.  All other profiles defined
   in this document are strict supersets of Core.

6.2.  Long-Horizon Profile

   The Long-Horizon profile is designed for receipts that must remain
   verifiable after a cryptographically-relevant quantum computer
   (CRQC) becomes available.  Current NIST and NSA guidance (CNSA 2.0)
   targets a migration deadline of 2035 for national-security systems.

   In addition to all Core requirements, the Long-Horizon profile:

   o  REQUIRES a composite ML-DSA-65+Ed25519 signature entry in the
      additional_signatures array, using the algorithm identifier
      "Composite-ML-DSA-65+Ed25519" as defined in
      [I-D.ietf-lamps-pq-composite-sigs].

   o  REQUIRES a standalone ML-DSA-65 entry for verifiers that do
      not support composite signatures.

   o  REQUIRES the receipt to carry "profile": "LongHorizon-v1" in
      the profile field.

   Core verifiers MUST NOT reject Long-Horizon receipts; they MUST
   treat unrecognized additional_signatures entries as unknown
   extensions and ignore them.

6.3.  High-Assurance Profile

   The High-Assurance profile is designed for legal-submission, court-
   admissibility, BFSI, and regulatory-compliance use cases.  In
   addition to all Core requirements, this profile:

   o  REQUIRES hardware-backed signing (HSM or FIPS 140-3 Level 3).

   o  REQUIRES anchor window <= 60 minutes.

   o  REQUIRES inclusion in at least two independent calendar
      operators' Merkle trees.

   o  REQUIRES the receipt to carry "profile": "HighAssurance-v1".


7.  SCITT Mapping

   LPR receipts map to the IETF SCITT architecture
   [I-D.ietf-scitt-architecture] as follows:

      SCITT concept            LPR equivalent
      -----------------------  -----------------------------------------
      Statement                LPR receipt (the signed CBOR object)
      Envelope                 The receipt's outer CBOR map
      Transparency Log         Bitcoin blockchain (OP_RETURN entries)
      Transparency Service     Foundation calendar operator (or any
                               independent operator per Section 5.5
                               of the LPR specification)
      Receipt (SCITT sense)    The anchor object (Merkle inclusion
                               proof + btc_txid + btc_block_height)
      Verifier                 Any party with a Bitcoin node and
                               possession of the receipt

   This mapping allows SCITT-compliant verification tooling to process
   LPR receipts by adapting the Transparency Log substrate from a
   conventional Certificate Transparency log to the Bitcoin blockchain.
   No changes to SCITT statement semantics are required.


8.  Verification

   A receipt is valid if and only if all of the following checks
   pass:

   1.  The canonical CBOR encoding of the receipt with signature and
       anchor omitted is recomputed, and the Ed25519 signature in
       signature.sig_bytes verifies against signature.signer_pubkey
       for that encoded byte string ([RFC8032]).

   2.  The asserted artifact.content_hash is recomputed by hashing
       the canonical artifact bytes and matches the value in the
       receipt.

   3.  If chain.prev_receipt_hash is non-null, the predecessor
       receipt is independently fetched, its canonical CBOR is
       computed (including its signature and anchor fields), and the
       resulting SHA-256 matches chain.prev_receipt_hash.

   4.  If anchor.anchor_status is "ANCHORED", the Merkle inclusion
       proof in anchor.merkle_path is verified to demonstrate
       inclusion of anchor.merkle_leaf_hash in a tree whose root
       appears, prefixed by "LPR1", in the OP_RETURN output of the
       Bitcoin transaction at anchor.btc_txid, included in the block
       at anchor.btc_block_height.

   Verifiers MUST reject receipts whose timestamp_ns is later than
   the Bitcoin block time of the asserted anchor.  This bound
   prevents predated assertions.


9.  Security Considerations

   LPR v1.0 relies on the collision resistance of SHA-256 and the
   signature security of Ed25519.  Both primitives have no known
   practical attacks at the time of publication.  Migration paths to
   post-quantum primitives are provided in the Long-Horizon profile
   (Section 6.2), which adds composite ML-DSA-65+Ed25519 signatures
   [I-D.ietf-lamps-pq-composite-sigs] for receipts requiring
   verifiability beyond the estimated CRQC threat horizon.

   The security of an LPR receipt against authorship spoofing
   reduces to the secrecy of the signer's Ed25519 private key.
   Signers handling high-value receipts SHOULD use hardware-backed
   key storage.

   Implementations MUST use constant-time Ed25519 signature
   verification to mitigate timing side-channel attacks.

   The artifact.content_hash field is the hash of an off-chain
   artifact.  Implementers handling personal data MUST evaluate
   whether the hash itself constitutes personal data under
   applicable data-protection law, including the European Union
   General Data Protection Regulation and successor regimes.
   Receipts SHOULD NOT contain personal data in any field beyond the
   hash; implementer-operated off-chain storage SHOULD support
   deletion of the underlying artifact in response to GDPR Article
   17 requests, in which case the on-chain receipt continues to
   exist but references a hash for which the underlying artifact is
   no longer available.

   Denial-of-service against an operator-run calendar server is
   possible.  Implementations SHOULD permit operation of redundant
   calendars and SHOULD NOT make verification dependent on a single
   calendar's availability.
