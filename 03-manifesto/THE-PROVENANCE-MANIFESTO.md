## I · The state of the record

Every institution that produces a document, signs a contract, decides a case, files a return, publishes a study, releases a model, or issues a ruling now does so in an environment where the line between an authentic record and a synthetic one is no longer self-evident. Documents can be altered after the fact and the alteration left invisible. Generative systems produce material indistinguishable from human-authored work. Metadata that once tracked authorship is stripped on every social platform within seconds of upload. Court records, regulatory filings, scientific papers, journalistic accounts, financial disclosures, medical records, and the safety documentation of the artificial-intelligence systems now embedded in every consequential decision — all of these have arrived at the same condition. **The record exists. Its integrity does not.**

This is not a future problem. It is the present condition of the documentary economy. The cost of being wrong about whether a document is genuine has, in the last twenty-four months, become measurable in court verdicts, regulatory penalties, market reactions, electoral outcomes, and lives.

## II · What we believe

We hold these convictions plainly.

**One.** A document's existence at a moment in time, its authorship, and the chain of its modifications must be provable to any party, with no requirement to trust the document's holder, its custodian, its software vendor, or any single authority.

**Two.** The mechanism of proof must outlast every commercial entity, every software vendor, every government, and every standards body involved in producing the document. The verification of a document in 2126 must not depend on the survival of an institution that exists in 2026.

**Three.** The act of marking a document with proof must be free for any human and any institution that wishes to mark one. Civic infrastructure does not charge tolls.

**Four.** Artificial intelligence does not invalidate the record. It clarifies the question. A system that produces a billion sentences a day requires the same answer to *when was this written, by whom, and has it been altered* as any other producer of language. The answer must be the same answer.

**Five.** The integrity of the record is not a feature of any one product. It is a property of the public infrastructure on which records are made.

## III · What we are doing

We are publishing today, under the open license of the LedgerProof Foundation, the **LedgerProof Receipt — LPR 1.0 specification**: a public, cryptographically sound, vendor-neutral format for binding an authorship statement, a timestamp, and a Merkle-aggregated proof to a record that is then anchored on the Bitcoin blockchain. The specification is free to implement. The verifier is free to use. The protocol depends on no single company or government for its continued operation. The reference implementations in Python and TypeScript are MIT-licensed.

We are submitting the specification to the Internet Engineering Task Force as an Internet-Draft under standard process.

We are contributing the LPR receipt format to the verifiable-AI-provenance work of the VeritasChain Standards Organization, the content-credentials work of the Coalition for Content Provenance and Authenticity, and the agent-tool-protocol work of the Model Context Protocol community, as a composable trust root those efforts can rely upon.

The receipts that anchor this Manifesto, the LPR 1.0 specification, the LedgerProof Foundation Charter, and the inaugural Founding Whitepaper are all present in a single Bitcoin transaction on the day this Manifesto is published. *The act of declaring this Manifesto demonstrates the protocol it describes.*

## IV · What we are asking

We are not asking for funding. We are not asking for endorsement. We are not asking any individual or institution to adopt anything.

We are asking the institutions whose records matter — the courts, the regulators, the auditors, the journalists, the universities, the laboratories, the publishers, the banks, the standards bodies — to read the specification, to verify the receipts, and to consider whether the integrity of their own records would be better served by an open public infrastructure than by the patchwork of vendor-specific proofs the documentary economy has so far accepted.

The infrastructure is here. The protocol is open. The verifier is free. The record can be made provable. The cost of doing so is now smaller than the cost of being wrong.
