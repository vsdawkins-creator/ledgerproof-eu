## 2. Foundation Independence FAQ

The FAQ is a generic Q&A document designed to be released publicly on the Foundation website on or before Jul 6 and to be quoted directly to press in response to ownership-structure or governance-influence questions. The fourteen questions below are pre-approved. Veronica reviews any deviation. The Foundation General Counsel signs off on legal accuracy before public release.

### Q1 — How is the LedgerProof Foundation funded?

The LedgerProof Foundation is a United States 501(c)(3) public charity organized in Delaware, with a wholly-Foundation-controlled Dutch Stichting (Amsterdam) serving as the EU contractual subsidiary. The Foundation's charitable-purpose mandate is to maintain the open LedgerProof Protocol as public-interest infrastructure for AI transparency. The Foundation is funded by three independent revenue streams: (a) an IP license fee from LedgerProof Inc., the commercial implementation entity, set at fair-market terms reviewed annually by an independent valuation firm; (b) grant funding from research institutions and public-interest technology funders; (c) member-Foundation dues from enterprise participants, which are capped per entity to prevent any single member from contributing more than 15% of annual Foundation revenue. Foundation financial statements are published annually under IRS Form 990 disclosure requirements and the Dutch Stichting under its own transparency obligations; both filings are available on the Foundation website. The Foundation maintains a six-month operating reserve independent of any single funding source.

### Q2 — Who owns the LedgerProof Protocol IP?

The LedgerProof Protocol — including the specification, reference implementations, cryptographic schemes, and trademark — is owned by the LedgerProof Foundation under an irrevocable transfer executed at Foundation incorporation. The Foundation grants LedgerProof Inc. a non-exclusive, perpetual license to implement and commercialize products using the protocol. The license is non-exclusive by design: any third party — including competitors of LedgerProof Inc. — may implement the protocol under the same terms. The Foundation cannot revoke the license to LedgerProof Inc. but also cannot grant exclusivity to LedgerProof Inc. or to any other entity. The protocol specification is published under a permissive open-source license that permits commercial use, modification, and redistribution.

### Q3 — What is the relationship between LedgerProof Foundation and LedgerProof Inc.?

The Foundation and Inc. are legally and operationally distinct. The Foundation is the IP owner and standards steward. LedgerProof Inc. is the primary commercial implementer of the protocol but holds no governance role in the Foundation. The Foundation Board includes no LedgerProof Inc. employees, officers, or directors. The Foundation maintains its own legal counsel, banking, accounting, and audit relationships separate from Inc. Shared services (technology infrastructure, real estate) are not used between the two entities. Inter-entity transactions — the IP license fee, any sponsorship arrangements — are governed by a written IP Transaction Committee Charter requiring independent-director approval and arms-length terms documentation.

### Q4 — Is the protocol governance influenced by any single enterprise customer?

No. The Foundation Board operates under a Conflict of Interest Policy that requires any board member with a material commercial relationship to a protocol decision to recuse from deliberation on that decision. Protocol specification changes require a documented public comment period, Technical Steering Committee review, and Foundation Board approval — a three-stage process that no single customer can shortcut. Foundation Advisory Council members, who include enterprise representatives, hold no voting authority on protocol or governance decisions. The Advisory Council is consultative only. This structural separation between commercial Advisory voice and fiduciary Board decision is documented in the Foundation Bylaws and the Advisory Council Charter.

### Q5 — Can a foreign-owned enterprise influence Foundation decisions?

No mechanism exists by which enterprise ownership structure — domestic or foreign — translates into Foundation governance influence. Enterprise customers of the protocol may apply to join the Foundation Advisory Council, which is consultative. They do not vote on Foundation Board matters. They do not appoint Foundation Board members. They do not approve protocol specification changes. The Foundation has adopted a policy that no Advisory Council seat will be granted to any entity whose ownership structure raises concerns under EU foreign-investment screening regulations or comparable jurisdictional review frameworks, with the Foundation General Counsel making the determination case-by-case in consultation with external Dutch and EU regulatory counsel.

### Q6 — How does Foundation board composition prevent capture?

The Foundation Board has seven voting seats: three independent technical members (selected for protocol-engineering credibility and absence of commercial conflict), two independent governance members (selected for regulatory and public-interest credentials), one academic seat, and one rotating civil-society seat. No seat is held by a commercial customer or commercial vendor. Board members serve staggered three-year terms, with a maximum of two consecutive terms, ensuring continuous turnover and preventing entrenchment. Board succession is governed by a Nominating Committee composed of independent members only. The Foundation Bylaws prevent any single nominator from holding more than one seat on the Nominating Committee at any time.

### Q7 — What is the role of the Foundation Advisory Council versus the Foundation Board?

The Foundation Board holds all fiduciary, governance, and decision authority. The Advisory Council is consultative — it provides input on protocol roadmap, real-world deployment considerations, and standards-body coordination. Advisory Council members do not vote on Foundation matters, do not approve budgets, do not select board members, and do not approve protocol specification changes. The Advisory Council exists so that enterprise deployers, civil-society advocates, and regulatory observers can be heard structurally rather than informally. The distinction between Advisory Council seat (commercial relationship acceptable) and Board seat (commercial relationship disqualifying) is the central capture-resistance design of the Foundation governance structure.

### Q8 — Why did the Foundation choose Bitcoin as the anchor substrate?

The Foundation selected Bitcoin as the cryptographic anchor substrate for three reasons. First, Bitcoin offers the longest continuous record of public, censorship-resistant timestamping available — over 16 years of uninterrupted block production with no successful chain-reorganization beyond a small number of blocks. Second, Bitcoin's proof-of-work consensus is computationally infeasible for any nation-state or coalition to forge retroactively at the cost level relevant to AI-compliance evidence preservation. Third, Bitcoin's protocol stability — its resistance to feature additions and consensus changes — is itself a guarantee that the anchor substrate will not be modified in ways that invalidate historical receipts. The Foundation periodically reviews anchor-substrate choice and could in principle add additional substrates, but the choice of Bitcoin as the primary anchor reflects technical, not ideological, judgment.

### Q9 — What guarantees offline verifiability of receipts?

A LedgerProof receipt is verifiable using only three inputs: the Merkle inclusion proof for the receipt, an Ed25519 signature over the proof, and a Bitcoin block header from a publicly available Bitcoin node. Verification requires no connection to the LedgerProof Foundation, no connection to LedgerProof Inc., no connection to any LedgerProof-operated infrastructure. The reference verifier is open-source, fewer than 500 lines of code, and has been audited. A receipt verified today will be verifiable in twenty years using the same three inputs and a Bitcoin node from that time. The Foundation operates optional verification convenience nodes for parties who do not wish to run their own Bitcoin node, but these are convenience services — not requirements. The cryptographic guarantee is independent of the Foundation's continued existence.

### Q10 — How does the Foundation handle conflicts between Inc. commercial interests and Foundation public-interest mandate?

The Foundation Bylaws explicitly enumerate the Foundation's charitable purpose as the maintenance of the protocol as public-interest infrastructure. When a Foundation decision could plausibly advance Inc. commercial interests at the expense of the public-interest mandate, the Conflict of Interest Policy requires the matter to be flagged in advance, presented with an independent staff analysis, and decided by independent board members only. The Foundation General Counsel maintains a public docket of all such flagged decisions, with anonymized summaries published in the annual transparency report. To date, the Foundation has structured its IP license to Inc., its trademark sublicensing policy, and its standards-body participation framework specifically to limit the circumstances in which such conflicts arise. The Foundation is also a member of the IETF and contributes to the SCITT Working Group as a neutral steward.

### Q11 — Is the protocol available to any enterprise globally?

Yes, subject to applicable export-control and sanctions law. The protocol specification is published openly. Anyone may implement it. The Foundation does not gate access to the specification, to reference implementations, or to verification infrastructure. The Foundation does maintain a sanctioned-entity list aligned with US Treasury OFAC, EU consolidated sanctions, and UK OFSI lists; Foundation services (Advisory Council membership, training resources, formal recognition programs) are unavailable to entities on those lists. The protocol itself, as open-source software, is governed by the export-control treatment of the software license, not by any Foundation gating mechanism.

### Q12 — Which jurisdictions does the Foundation operate in?

The Foundation operates as a multi-jurisdictional structure: (a) the parent entity is a United States 501(c)(3) public charity organized in Delaware, with counsel through Adler & Colvin (San Francisco) and IRS Form 1023 determination in process; (b) the Dutch Stichting EU subsidiary, registered with KvK Amsterdam under NautaDutilh counsel, serves as the European contractual counterparty for Foundation activities including standards-body participation and EU enterprise engagements; (c) additional planned subsidiaries — a Swiss Verein (Zurich, target Q1 2027) for IP stewardship per the Linux Foundation precedent, and a Singapore non-profit (target H1 2027) for Asia-Pacific reach. LedgerProof Inc., the commercial implementer, is separately incorporated as a Delaware C-corporation and is jurisdictionally distinct from the Foundation in all four locations. Foundation banking, audit, and legal counsel are established independently within each jurisdiction.

### Q13 — How are Foundation governance changes anchored as public receipts?

The Foundation eats its own dog food. Every material Foundation governance decision — Bylaws amendments, Board appointments, IP license modifications, annual budget approvals, protocol specification version releases — is published with an associated LedgerProof receipt that anchors a content hash of the decision text to the Bitcoin blockchain. Any party can verify, at any future date, that a published Foundation decision has not been altered after publication. The Foundation publishes a real-time governance ledger on its website with each decision linked to its receipt. This is the same evidence mechanism the Foundation provides to enterprise customers, applied recursively to its own operations.

### Q14 — What happens if a customer's parent company is in a jurisdiction with regulatory concerns?

Customer ownership structure is reviewed by the LedgerProof Inc. customer-onboarding compliance function under a documented policy that considers foreign-investment-screening determinations, sanctions exposure, and jurisdictional review frameworks. The compliance review is independent of revenue interests. Customers may use the protocol regardless of ownership structure because the protocol is openly available, but membership in formal Foundation governance bodies — including the Advisory Council — is subject to the governance-eligibility screen described in Q5. The Foundation maintains the right to decline Advisory Council membership for any entity whose ownership structure raises concerns under applicable screening frameworks, and the Foundation publishes the aggregate number of such declinations in its annual transparency report without naming the specific entities.

The response accomplishes three things: it acknowledges the question without engaging it, it asserts a defensible principle (customer-reference timing is customer-controlled), and it redirects to Foundation governance — the topic on which Veronica is most credible and on which the FAQ above provides comprehensive backup.

### 3.2 The four-hour rule

When a journalist's question touches on a specific customer or that customer's parent or affiliated entities, Veronica's response is always:

> "Let me get back to you within the hour."

She then immediately Signal-messages the Brunswick lead with the journalist name, outlet, exact question, and deadline. Brunswick responds within 30 minutes with the recommended written response. Veronica reviews, edits if needed, and replies to the journalist within 4 hours.

The four-hour rule exists because Veronica's first instinct will be to answer substantively in the moment. That instinct is wrong in this scenario. Brunswick's role is to slow the loop just enough that the response is composed, not reactive.

### 3.3 Off-limits topics

Veronica does not discuss the following in any setting — on the record, on background, off the record, with friendly journalists, or in social conversation that could be later attributed:

- Any specific customer's ownership structure.
- The internal sequencing of customer commitments (who signed first, who was approached when).
- The fact that counterweight outreach was a deliberate strategy.
- The cost or scope of Brunswick's engagement.
- The existence of this document.

"Off the record" with a journalist is not a safe channel for these topics. Journalists honor off-the-record reliably for sources they need to protect; they do not always honor it for sources they are competing to break. The cost of a single slip on any of the above is catastrophic and not recoverable.
